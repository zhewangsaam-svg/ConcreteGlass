Case Study Report

Concrete and Glass: Performance and Maintenance Case Study

This report presents a case study of Darma Tower, a landmark high-rise residential development in London's Nine Elms regeneration area. It comprises two interconnected towers—a 50-story, approximately 170-meter-tall structure and a 23-story tower—housing 360 luxury apartments. The podium level features multifunctional commercial and leisure spaces. Completed in 2022, the building is a significant component of London's contemporary skyline.

Primarily residential in function, the structure employs a reinforced concrete frame with a glass curtain wall envelope system (British Standards Institution, 2013; British Standards Institution, 2016a). The building adopts a modern minimalist aesthetic, characterized by its concrete structure and glass envelope, tailored to meet the functional demands of high-density living.

2. Material Selection Criteria

2.1 Selection of Concrete

Taking the Damac Tower high-rise residential building in London as an example, this multi-story residential structure requires a design capable of enduring long-term vertical loads (such as floor slab dead weight, furniture, and occupant loads) while primarily controlling horizontal forces from wind action.

In such high-rise residences, reinforced concrete structures offer high load-bearing capacity and overall stiffness. This effectively controls inter-story lateral displacement and vibration comfort, meeting the stress and deformation requirements for primary components like columns, walls, cores, and floor slabs.

Regarding fire resistance, concrete's non-combustible nature, combined with structural controls like member dimensions and protective layers, facilitates achieving the required fire resistance ratings for residential buildings.

In terms of durability and maintenance, concrete benefits from a mature supply chain and relatively controllable costs. Through proper protective layers, waterproofing, and detailing, it maintains excellent durability and structural stability in London's rainy, humid climate, significantly reducing long-term maintenance expenses.

Consequently, concrete was selected as the core structural material to balance structural safety, construction efficiency, and long-term asset value.

2.2 Glass Selection

The extensive use of glass centers on functionality and aesthetics: glass curtain walls maximize natural daylight to create bright living environments; double- or triple-pane glass units ensure adequate lighting while meeting thermal and acoustic regulations.

Functionally, the glass envelope system significantly improves indoor lighting and reduces artificial lighting energy consumption. Double-pane insulated glass provides both thermal insulation and soundproofing, meeting modern apartment comfort requirements.

Aesthetically, glass curtain walls create a clean, contemporary appearance that harmonizes with surrounding architectural styles. Glass partitions in internal common areas enhance transparency and visual openness.

For safety, tempered and laminated safety glass complies with high-density residential standards, preventing injuries from broken glass.

London's climate is generally mild with relatively frequent rainfall, where wind typically dominates horizontal forces on high-rise buildings. Under these environmental and usage conditions, critical control points for concrete components generally focus on service performance (deflection/crack control), construction quality (vibration and curing), and durability with joint sealing continuity in humid environments.

Indoor areas maintain relatively stable temperature and humidity, yet columns, beams, and floor slabs must still bear self-weight and live loads while meeting deformation and crack control requirements during normal service life. Inadequate compaction during construction or insufficient curing can lead to surface defects like honeycombing and bugholes , compromising durability.

Basements and similar spaces are more prone to humid conditions. Particular attention should be given to the integrity of closures and surface protection at details such as slab undersides and joints/sealing lines.

Exterior envelope components are directly exposed to rain and wind. The continuity of construction joints and node seals significantly impacts water infiltration risks and the thermal performance of the envelope structure. Therefore, material durability and node detailing practices should be controlled and verified according to applicable code requirements.

4. Maintenance or performance issues

4.1 Concrete Repair

(a)

Field photographs reveal continuous linear sealing lines beneath the basement slab, with vertical sealing strips visible at wall-column junctions. Walls exhibit visible traces of surface treatment, including regular hole plugging and localized patching.

Given London's relatively humid environment and prolonged usage conditions, discontinuities in these sealing lines or localized coating failures may allow moisture migration along interfaces, leading to localized damp patches. This accelerates coating chalking, edge lifting, and increased demand for secondary repairs. Such issues primarily manifest as compromised durability and elevated maintenance frequency, without directly indicating structural safety concerns based on current observations.

Conclusion

This report analyzes two core materials—concrete and glass—used in London apartments, drawing the following key conclusions:

Material selection for apartments must rigorously balance structural safety, functional comfort, and cost control. Concrete frame structures meet the load-bearing and fireproofing requirements of multi-story buildings, while glass envelope systems achieve functional objectives of daylighting, thermal insulation, and soundproofing.

The combined application of these materials represents one of the optimal solutions for high-density residential scenarios in apartments. Maintenance issues with building materials are often linked to construction quality and environmental aging.

Problems like concrete cracking require standardized construction techniques and regular maintenance to extend the building's lifespan. This case study demonstrates that precisely matching material selection to performance requirements is central to construction engineering.

Only by integrating building function, regional climate, and usage scenarios can structural safety, residential comfort, and durability be unified. Field observations reveal that even after meticulous design and construction, material performance degradation remains an inevitable long-term challenge.
